VOICES OF TIBET

Before morning breaks in the Valley of the Kings (Tombs of Tibetan Kings), Tenzin Phuntsok’s day begins around the ancient tombs.The 25-year-oldmonk, together with 68-year-old Jampa Choesang, are guardians of the biggest mausoleum found in Tibet, located at the southern bank of the Yarlung Zangbo River in Shannan Prefecture.

From dawn to dusk, their lives echo the illuminated ghee lamps of the temple.

A 25-YEAR-OLD’S LIFE CHOICE

Lighting ghee lamps at the ancient temple atop the historically recorded Songtsan Gambo’s tomb is the first thing for Tenzin to do in the morning.
After that, he cleans and picks up trash around tombs, burns incense and prepares holy water in the temple, chants his sutras, then comes the most challenging duty for him: showing visitors around the temple. Coming to work in the valley in 2014, Tenzin is trying hard to learn the history of the tombs and pass it on to visitors from around the world, while overcoming his introverted nature.

Tenzin feels fulfilled through keeping focus on maintaining the place.

“I’ve worshipped and believed in Buddhism since I was very young. I really enjoy learning sutras. ” Tenzin cultivated his faith of Buddhism from childhood, and his beliefs lead Tenzin to the valley.

Born in 1990, and part of a generation defined by a society with an unstable working attitude, Tenzin was described by his father as simple-minded. Before coming to guard the tombs, Tenzin was working in a construction site and ended up performing the same amount of work as five people. He was known to devote himself to whatever he was doing without complaining.

Tenzin cultivated his faith of Buddhism from childhood, and his beliefs lead Tenzin to the valley.

Worried about his health, Tenzin’s father had a long talk with him and decided the quiet temple of the Tibetan king tombs is the place which suited his personality and it would give him a chance to get close to what he loved from childhood.

“I really like this place and barely think of going back home. My parents would feel happy for me if I do my job well here. ” Tenzin soon adapted to the simple life in the temple and set up his mind to guard the tombs in the valley for the rest of his life.

TWO MEN GUARD HISTORY

Before Tenzin came to the valley, Jampa Choesang was the only guard responsible for the three million square kilometers of tombs.

“There were four seniors guarding the place a couple of years ago. But as time passed, some of them passed away and some got health problems. Then, it was only me.” 68-year-old Jampa Choesang was the cook in the temple before taking over guard duty. “Tenzin has passed through the month-long assessment from local officials and proved himself a very responsible young man.”

Tenzin Phuntsok and Jampa Choesang are guardians of the biggest mausoleum found in Tibet.

Now with Tenzin on the team, the two complement each other’s characteristics. “Teacher Jampa is a talkative man and is always taking care of me, so I don’t feel lonely here,” Tenzin said.


The two are in charge of managing the temple above Songtsan Gambo’s tomb and guarding the other Tibetan Kings’ tombs. They take turns guarding the entire area at least ten times a month.

The development of funeral culture in Tibet

According to Tibetan history books such as, “Grand Ceremonies of the Wise,” “Chronicle of Tibetan Kings,” and “Officials,” only 16 of the 21 tomb occupants have been identified. Among them, Songtsan Gambo’s tomb is the most famous one, which attracts loads of visitors and worshipers.

When there are no people visiting, Tenzin and Jampa take a seat in front of the temple looking at the mausoleums built on the birthplace of the ancient Tibetan nationality. No one can guarantee spending the whole life with the tombs, but this is how the responsibility of guarding history is passed from one generation to the next.
